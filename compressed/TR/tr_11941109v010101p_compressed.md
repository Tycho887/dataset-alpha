# ETSI TR 119 411-9: Electronic Signatures and Trust Infrastructures (ESI); Policy and security requirements for Trust Service Providers issuing certificates; Part 9: Requirements on a Certificate Transparency (CT) Ecosystem to make the issuing of certificates transparent and verifiable
**Source**: ETSI | **Version**: V1.1.1 | **Date**: 2026-03 | **Type**: Informative (Technical Report)
**Original**: ETSI TR 119 411-9 V1.1.1 (2026-03)

## Scope (Summary)
This document provides an overview of Certificate Transparency (CT) technology and its application to ensure transparency and traceability in certificate issuance. It maps the CT ecosystem, reviews existing standards and initiatives, and provides guidance on how to define a European CT infrastructure for certificates issued under Regulation (EU) No 910/2014 (eIDAS) as amended by Regulation (EU) No 2024/1183. It also uses the WebPKI case to illustrate CT functionality and proposes recommendations for future standardization.

## 4 Certificate Transparency
### 4.1 Introduction
CT is an Internet security framework that enhances PKI integrity, accountability, and transparency by requiring that all issued digital certificates be publicly recorded in cryptographically verifiable, append-only logs. CT is currently deployed exclusively within the WebPKI.

### 4.2 Objectives of CT
- **a) Detection of certificate misissuance**: CT enables timely identification of incorrectly or fraudulently issued certificates.
- **b) Enhancement of transparency within the PKI**: Publication of certificate issuance events in verifiable logs increases transparency.
- **c) Accountability of Trust Service Providers**: TSPs acting as CAs must submit all issued certificates to public logs, enabling independent review.
- **d) Support for third-party certificate monitoring**: Credential owners and others can monitor issuance for early detection of unauthorized certificates.
- **e) Facilitation of revocation and incident response**: Real-time log data enables efficient incident detection and revocation.
- **f) Mitigation of undetected use of fraudulent certificates**: Pre-use logging limits MITM attacks and impersonation.

### 4.3 Functionality and participants in the CT ecosystem
#### 4.3.1 Functional Overview
- **a) Certificate Submission**: TSP-CA submits certificate or precertificate to CT logs.
- **b) SCT Issuance**: Log issues a Signed Certificate Timestamp (SCT) – a cryptographic commitment for inclusion within Maximum Merge Delay (MMD).
- **c) SCT Delivery Mechanisms**: Embedded in X.509 certificate, via TLS SCT extension, or stapled in OCSP response.
- **d) Log Structure and Verifiability**: Logs use append-only Merkle Hash Trees, providing inclusion and consistency proofs.
- **e) Client Enforcement**: Relying parties (e.g. browsers) validate SCTs; certificates not meeting CT policy may be rejected.

#### 4.3.2 Ecosystem Roles
- **a) TSP-CA**: Issues certificates, submits to logs, obtains SCTs, ensures delivery.
- **b) CT Log Operator**: Maintains logs; responsible for performance, availability, correctness; may be subject to qualification and audit.
- **c) Relying Party (Client)**: Validates SCTs during certificate verification; enforces CT compliance.
- **d) Monitor**: Observes logs for specific certificate entries; notifies owners of unexpected issuance.
- **e) Auditor**: Verifies cryptographic structure and behaviour of logs; ensures append-only integrity.

### 4.4 Policies, norms, standards and initiatives
#### 4.4.1 Policies
Trust and interoperability will be ensured by common policies on all ecosystem roles.

#### 4.4.2 Norms and standards
- **IETF RFC 6962** [i.7]: Experimental protocol for publicly logging TLS certificates; defines SCT, precertificates, and inclusion proofs.
- **IETF RFC 9162** [i.8]: Version 2.0 of CT protocol; obsoletes RFC 6962; specifies new TLS extension for CT artifacts (limited deployment).

#### 4.4.3 Initiatives
- **Static-CT-API (tiled logs)**: Experimental Chromium-based extension using tiled logs for inclusion-before-SCT and cacheable read path. Not IETF/ETSI standardized; managed by Community Cryptography Specification Project. As of 2025, Chrome accepts SCTs from Static-CT-API logs alongside RFC 6962 logs. Should be considered a vendor-driven initiative in early deployment.

## 5 Certificate Transparency
### 5.1 Use of CT with TLS
#### 5.1.1 Introduction
CT is a mandatory policy component for publicly trusted CAs issuing TLS server authentication certificates.

#### 5.1.2 Precertificate and SCT Workflow
- **a) Precertificate Generation**: CA generates a precertificate with CT Poison extension (not valid for TLS).
- **b) Submission to CT Logs**: Precertificate submitted to public logs; returns SCT (hash, timestamp, signature).
- **c) Final Certificate Issuance**: CA issues final certificate with SCTs embedded, stapled, or via TLS extension.

#### 5.1.3 SCT Delivery Methods
- **Embedded SCT**: In X.509 extension (OID 1.3.6.1.4.1.11129.2.4.2).
- **OCSP Stapling**: SCTs included in OCSP response; server staples during TLS handshake.
- **TLS Extension (Dynamic SCT)**: Server delivers SCTs via `signed_certificate_timestamp` extension.
Clients must validate at least one SCT.

NOTE: "Static CT" in this document means embedded SCTs (RFC 6962/9162). Static-CT-API is a different concept (clause 4.4.3).

#### 5.1.4 Log Requirements and Client Validation
- Logs must be publicly accessible, append-only, Merkle Trees, provide inclusion/consistency proofs, meet MMD and availability thresholds.
- Clients parse SCTs, verify signatures against trusted logs, optionally check inclusion post-connection.

#### 5.1.5 Browser Enforcement Policies (informative, as of Aug 2025)
- **Chrome**: Enforces CT per Chrome CT Policy.
- **Safari**: Enforces CT since 2021; SCTs embedded/OCSP/TLS.
- **Firefox**: Enforces CT since Firefox 135 (Feb 2025).
- **Windows/Edge**: Optional CT validation; maintains CT Log Monitor list; possible enforcement extensions.

NOTE: Policies evolve; consult latest documentation.

### 5.2 CT Log Incidents - Observations, Causes
#### 5.2.1 Background
Logs are expected to incorporate entries within MMD and meet ≥99% availability over 90-day rolling window. Incidents have arisen from infrastructure faults, configuration issues, hardware errors. Recovery may require retirement.

#### 5.2.2 Incident taxonomy (operator-neutral)
- **A**: Submission errors / service unavailability.
- **B**: MMD breaches and inclusion gaps.
- **C**: Signing-key exposure risk (precautionary deactivation/retirement).
- **D**: Hardware-induced data corruption (single-bit flip).
- **E**: Datacentre-level outages.
- **F**: Storage exhaustion and database corruption.
- **G**: Consistency violations (inconsistent STHs).

#### 5.2.3 Chronology of representative incidents (anonymized)
- 2018-11-30: Submission failures (Class A) – resolved by failover.
- Q2 2019: Availability drop with MMD alarms (Class B) – operator investigation.
- May 2020: Signing-key exposure risk (Class C) – precautionary retirement.
- Mid-2021: Hardware bit-flip corrupts Merkle state (Class D) – shard retired.
- Nov 2023: Datacentre power loss (Class E) – recovered via DR.
- Jan 2024: Storage exhaustion → DB corruption → retirement (Class F).

Lifecycle note: Planned removal of expired shards is not an incident.

### 5.3 Summary
CT is mandatory for public TLS certificates, strengthening CA accountability and PKI integrity. Proper implementation and enforcement are essential.

## 6 Recommendations for the use of CT for certificates issued in accordance with Regulation (EU) No 910/2014
### 6.1 Transfer of the current use of CT to a CT ecosystem for eIDAS certificates
It is proposed to adopt the fundamental CT structure (roles, SCTs, proofs, operational controls) for eIDAS certificates, building on RFC 6962 with forward compatibility to RFC 9162. A European CT infrastructure should be established with governance, qualification, and audit frameworks to address eIDAS-specific requirements, operational resilience, and avoid external dependencies.

### 6.2 Recommendation for the use of existing standards, norms and initiatives
#### 6.2.1 Recommendation for the use of existing standards
It is proposed that implementers build and operate the CT ecosystem in conformity with IETF RFC 6962 [i.7] as the primary interoperability basis. Where feasible, maintain forward compatibility with IETF RFC 9162 [i.8].

#### 6.2.2 Recommendation to consider Static-CT-API (tiled logs)
It is proposed that Static-CT-API be considered as input to future standardization, due to operational advantages: inclusion-before-SCT issuance, static cacheable read path, improved resilience and archival verifiability.

### 6.3 Recommendation for the definition of formats, protocols, policies and security requirements
#### 6.3.1 Introduction
ETSI should develop role-specific policy frameworks for all functional roles in the CT ecosystem.

#### 6.3.2 Policy Framework for TSP-CAs
Should define: log selection criteria (trust anchors, qualification), SCT acquisition requirements (number/diversity), delivery mechanisms, precertificate handling, internal controls (monitoring SCT validity before issuance).

#### 6.3.3 Policy Framework for Relying Parties
Should define: transparent trust criteria (versioned CT policy), SCT verification rules, incident and privacy handling.

#### 6.3.4 Policy Framework for Supervisory Bodies
Should define: ecosystem governance and role obligations, CT log admission/lifecycle (registration, qualification, state transitions, delisting) with machine-readable publication, minimum measurement/reporting (availability, MMD, inclusion timeliness), coordination/escalation for incidents, change management and transparency.

#### 6.3.5 Policy Framework for CT Log Operators
Should define: log service behaviour (MMD, inclusion guarantees, integrity), cryptographic controls (algorithms, key lifecycle), audit procedures (frequency, scope, publication), operational controls (submission filtering, rate limiting, mitigation of malicious activity).

#### 6.3.6 Policy Framework for CT Monitors
Should define: scope and frequency of observation, alerting mechanisms, criteria for identifying relevant entries, data handling and protection practices.

#### 6.3.7 Policy Framework for CT Log Auditors
Should address: verification methodology for inclusion/consistency proofs, audit intervals/coverage, procedures for reporting and publication, processes for handling inconsistencies (notification, escalation).
