# ETSI TR 119 520-1: Electronic Signatures and Trust Infrastructures (ESI); Framework of ERDS/REM standards; Part 1: New (Q)ERDS/(Q)ERDSP standardization rationalized framework as a result of the new components brought by eIDAS2.0

## Scope (Summary)
Defines a new rationalized standardization framework for (Q)ERDS/(Q)ERDSP, incorporating an HTTP-based ERDS baseline (similar to the REM baseline), changes from eIDAS2.0 (EU Digital Identity Wallet, Electronic Attestations of Attributes), and emerging technologies. Includes recommendations for updating ETSI TR 119 000 and ETSI TR 119 001 and identifies impacts on policy and security requirements standards.

## Definitions and Abbreviations
- **Electronic Registered Delivery Service (ERDS)**: electronic service that transmits data between sender and recipients, provides evidence (proof of sending/receiving), and protects against loss, theft, damage or unauthorized alterations.
- **ERDS evidence**: data generated within ERDS to prove a certain event occurred at a certain time.
- **Electronic Registered Delivery Service Provider (ERDSP)**: trust service provider providing ERDS.
- **Qualified Electronic Registered Delivery Service (QERDS)**: as specified in eIDAS.
- **Qualified Electronic Registered Delivery Service Provider (QERDSP)**: trust service provider providing QERDS.
- **Recipient**: natural or legal person to whom user content is addressed.
- **Sender**: natural or legal person that submits user content.
- **Trust service component**: one part of the overall service of a TSP (defined in ETSI EN 319 403-1).

Key abbreviations: API, CAB, CSI, DNS, DNSSEC, EAA, EBSI, EDICG, EUDIW, GDPR, JAdES, JSON, PDL, QC, QEAA, QERDS, QERDSP, QES, QTSP, REM, TL, TLS, TSP, TTP, URI.

## 4. (Q)ERDS/(Q)ERDSP Inputs for a New Rationalized Standardization Framework

### 4.1 General
This document proposes a framework that harmonizes and reorganizes existing ERDS standards, extends the REM baseline to HTTP-based ERDS, and addresses eIDAS2.0 requirements (EU eWallet, EAA) and emerging technologies. Table 1 lists the 22 existing standards to be reviewed.

### 4.2 Cross‑Border Interoperability

#### 4.2.1 Introduction
To achieve cross‑border QERDS interoperability, a minimal baseline with modularity and a non‑orthogonality principle is required.

#### 4.2.2 ETSI ERDS Baseline
##### 4.2.2.1 General Requirements
Aligns with eIDAS2 Recital 24. Features: technological neutrality, modularity, pluggability, extensibility based on non‑orthogonality, QTSP compliance, full compatibility with as‑is software.

##### 4.2.2.2 Application to the Entire ERDS Standard
The REM baseline (ETSI EN 319 532‑4) is considered an instance of the ERDS baseline. Extending the baseline across all ERDS inherits interoperability and compliance benefits.

##### 4.2.2.3 General Needs Identified
Basic components: packaging (user content, metadata, evidence) and transport (enveloping, protocol, addressing, security). Figure 2 shows a lightweight layer with a Smart Wrap Service and an HTTP‑based Service.

##### 4.2.2.4 Packaging
Options: JSON, XML, ZIP, MIME, CUSTOM. Figure 4 illustrates ZIP packaging.

##### 4.2.2.5 Transport
Common Service Infrastructure (CSI) for addressing/routing/trusting; mechanisms for wrapping and securing transport metadata; REST (optional auto‑resume) and TLS (optional DNSSEC). May support detached evidence or PDL storage. Example in Annex B shows eIDAS2.0‑tailored configuration.

##### 4.2.2.6 ERDS Baseline and REM Baseline: Functional Relationships
REM can use HTTP/TLS transport because REM messages are separated from transport information. REM baseline is instantiated by selecting MIME packaging and HTTP transport (Figures 6‑8).

#### 4.2.3 Inheritances from REM Baseline
##### 4.2.3.1 General Considerations
REM baseline is an instance of ERDS baseline at the functional level.

##### 4.2.3.2 Editorial Relationships
Feedback: too many fragmented documents, lack of implementation detail, overly general specifications. REM baseline (EN 319 532‑4) addressed this with a compact structure (Annex B: reference map, Annex C: normative extracts, Annex D: best practices, ZIP with examples/XSDs). ERDS baseline should adopt a similar compact style, repeating information where natural.

#### 4.2.4 Cross‑Interoperability
The baseline supports grace‑interoperability (direct), claim‑interoperability (indirect), and mixed forms. The Custom Packaging option in the Smart Wrap Service enables integration of diverse systems by encapsulating user content, metadata, and evidence in generic blobs with pointers. Example: ERDS interoperating with REM via capability query and JSON/JAdES wrapping (Figures 9, 10).

### 4.3 Implications of eIDAS2 Regulation

#### 4.3.1 Impact of eIDAS2 on ERDS
New components (EUDI Wallet, EAA, NIS2 requirements) affect identification, authentication, technical standardization, relied‑party interoperation, and new trust services (validation, preservation, EAA issuance, electronic ledgers). Table A.1 provides a comprehensive mapping. Key areas listed:
- EUDIW and EAA identification.
- Impact on identity matching and authentication.
- Access to transaction log (EUDIW).
- New interoperability framework.
- eIDAS2 services that can be externalized (e.g., certificate validation, signature creation, EAA validation, data validation, electronic ledger recording).

### 4.4 Impact of Emerging Technologies and Models
Emerging technologies (e.g., permissioned distributed ledgers, smart contracts, hybrid mail) may transform ERDS/REM. Lack of standardization could lead to non‑interoperable implementations. ETSI TR 119 520‑2 provides concrete proposals.

### 4.5 New Policy and Security Requirements
NIS2 Directive (Article 21) imposes risk management, incident reporting, supply chain security, and accountability for essential entities (including QTSPs). eIDAS2 introduces dual regulation. Policy updates must incorporate NIS2 Article 21 and the new eIDAS2 provisions (e.g., authentication, EUDIW, EAA). See Table A.1 for detailed impact.

## 5. Proposal for a New (Q)ERDS/(Q)ERDSP Standardization Rationalized Framework

### 5.1 Introduction
Standards should be organized around trust service components: identification, advanced electronic signature/seal, evidence, time‑stamping, security transfer, and interoperability (transversal). Table 2 maps each component to existing standards. These components can be outsourced (e.g., identification service, signature/seal service, time‑stamping).

### 5.2 Proposal on Simplification and Rationalization
Table 3 details the proposed reorganization:
- **New framework**: Update ETSI TR 119 500 and ETSI SR 019 050.
- **Policy and security**: Merge EN 319 521, EN 319 522‑1, EN 319 531, EN 319 532‑1 into a single document (using the trust‑service‑component approach).
- **Evidence and data integrity**:
  - Evidence: new document covering clause 8 of EN 319 522‑2, clause 5 of EN 319 522‑3, JSON format, PDF evidence, issuance/storage/retrieval API, mapping to European interoperability framework.
  - Data structures: one document from EN 319 522‑2, except clauses 8 and 9.
  - Bindings: email binding (EN 319 532‑3), AS4 binding (one part from EN 319 522‑3 clause 4 and parts 4‑1, 4‑2, 4‑3 material), plus a binding for existing e‑delivery services to evolve transparently to ERDS/QERDS.
- **Interoperability**:
  - Framework: CSI and Trusted Lists (if not in policy).
  - Profiles: REM baseline (EN 319 532‑4) and a new ERDS HTTP‑based baseline.
  - Testing: merge conformance (TS 119 524‑1 + TS 119 534‑1) and interop test suites (TS 119 524‑2 + TS 119 534‑2), explicitly including baseline testing.

## Requirements Summary
The document is a Technical Report; the following key proposals are expressed as recommendations (should):

Provides a concrete configuration for the ERDS baseline transport module: CSI using Trusted Lists, JSON wrapping (RFC 7159), JAdES signing (TS 119 182‑1), optional PDL recording, REST with auto‑resume, TLS optionally augmented with DNSSEC.
