# ETSI TR 119 520-2: Electronic Signatures and Trust Infrastructures (ESI); Framework of ERDS/REM standards; Part 2: Impact of emerging technologies on ERDS/REM Models
**Source**: ETSI | **Version**: V1.1.1 | **Date**: 2024-04 | **Type**: Informative (Technical Report)
**Original**: DTR/ESI-0019520-2 (available at https://www.etsi.org/standards-search)

## Scope (Summary)
This Technical Report studies the impact of emerging technologies and models on ERDS/REM provision and assesses their impact on the ERDS framework of standards. It monitors identified technologies/models (clause 4) and proposes concrete actions (clause 5), with the resulting impact reflected in ETSI TR 119 520-1 [i.13].

## Definitions and Abbreviations
### Terms
For the purposes of the present document, the terms given in ETSI TR 119 001 [i.14], ETSI EN 319 521 [i.4], ETSI TR 119 520-1 [i.13] and the following apply:
- **DEP eDelivery**: set of technical specifications and standards for electronic message exchange developed by the Commission under CEF and continued under DEP.
- **eIDAS 2.0**: EU Regulation amending Regulation (EU) No 910/2014 [i.2] as regards establishing a framework for a European Digital Identity.
- **NIS2**: Directive (EU) 2022/2555 [i.15] on measures for a high common level of cybersecurity across the Union.
- **Registered Electronic Mail (REM)**: specific type of electronic registered delivery based on formats, protocols, and mechanisms used in ordinary electronic mail messaging.
- **ERDS**: Electronic Registered Delivery Service (as defined in ETSI EN 319 521 [i.4]).
- **QERDS**: Qualified Electronic Registered Delivery Service.
- **TTP**: Trusted Third Party.
- **TCE**: Trusted Communication Evidence.

## 4 Gathering information on emerging technologies and new models
### 4.1 Information sources
Monitoring focused on:
- Standardization activities by ETSI ISG PDL, ISO/TC 154, CEN/TC 331.
- European initiatives: laws/regulations, DEP eDelivery, Peppol, EBSI.

### 4.2 Monitoring of standardization activities
#### 4.2.1 ETSI ISG Permissioned Distributed Ledger (PDL)
- **ETSI GR PDL 014 [i.9]**: Describes PDL properties supporting non-repudiation of origin, emission, receipt, submission, delivery, transport. PDL can add trust/notarization to electronic delivery.
- **ETSI GR PDL 017 [i.10]**: Application of PDL as qualified electronic ledger and in support of eIDAS 2.0 trust services.

#### 4.2.2 ISO 19626 Trusted communication platforms for electronic documents
- **ISO 19626-3 [i.11]**: Guidelines for blockchain-based TCP system. TCP architecture: TTP identity directory, communication server, communication client, TCE repository.
- Feasibility conclusions:
  - **TTP identity directory with DLT**: feasible; DLT secures trustworthiness of identity info and shares via synchronization. Sensitive info may be stored in block or not shared.
  - **TCP communication server with DLT**: NOT feasible (document size up to hundreds of MB vs block size 1-4 MB; DLT exposes documents, violating confidentiality).
  - **TCP communication client with DLT**: NOT feasible.
  - **TCE repository with DLT**: feasible; DLT ensures immutability, eliminates inconsistencies, reinforces non-repudiability.
- Implementation: private permissioned DLT system; all operations limited to TCP users. Major functions: smart contracts, blockchain functions, distributed ledger.

#### 4.2.3 CEN/TS 16326 Functional Specification for postal registered electronic mail
- **CEN/TS 16326:2013 [i.12]**: Based on superseded ETSI TS 102 640-1 [i.19]; references pre-eIDAS signature formats.
- ETSI TC ESI offers support to align with current ERDS/REM standards and to better support hybrid mail.

### 4.3 Monitoring of European initiatives
#### 4.3.1 Policy initiatives relevant for electronic delivery
##### 4.3.1.1 Single Digital Gateway, the once-only technical system
- **Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2022/1463 [i.5]** mandates OOTS. Requires compliance to electronic registered delivery services and utilization of DEP eDelivery. eDelivery Access Points must form a network; AS4 messaging protocol and ancillary services (overlapping with ISO 15000-2 [i.17]) are used.

##### 4.3.1.2 The EIF and the Interoperable Europe Act
- **Regulation (EU) 2024/903 [i.3]** (Interoperable Europe Act) includes the European Interoperable Framework (EIF). EIF layered approach: Legal, Organizational, Semantic, Technical interoperability.
- Relevance to ERDS: guides cross-border functionality, standardization, compatibility, legal alignment, user-centricity, innovation. Semantic and technical interoperability align with ERDS separation of evidence semantics and data formats.

##### 4.3.1.3 The eFTI draft implementing act
- Draft Commission Implementing Regulation for eFTI (Regulation (EU) 2020/1056 [i.16]) mandates use of DEP eDelivery for cross-border network.

#### 4.3.2 The European projects
##### 4.3.2.1 The Digital Europe Programme
- DEP maintains former CEF eDelivery (now DEP eDelivery), based on OASIS ebMS3 and AS4 (ISO 15000-1 [i.20], ISO 15000-2 [i.18]).
- Two identified initiatives (not yet part of official DEP eDelivery):
  - REST API Profile for light-weight communication; open source pilot available.
  - Pilot for connecting DEP eDelivery with EBSI: two use cases (1) whitelist for easier node addition, (2) notarizing timestamps of eDelivery messages on blockchain.

##### 4.3.2.2 Peppol
- Originally EU-funded project, now international association. Network based on AS4. Aim: enable electronic communication between businesses and public sector in procurement. Gaining acceptance outside EU.

### 4.4 ERDS/REM Emerging technologies
#### 4.4.1 Blockchain & DLT
- DLT provides decentralized, secure method for storing/verifying information, enhancing security and reliability of ERDS evidence (proof of sending/receiving, protection against loss/theft/unauthorized alterations).
- DLT is maintained by network of nodes; not controlled by single party.
- Standardization needed to ensure interoperability between DLT and ERDS implementations. Compliance with privacy rules (e.g., right to be forgotten) may be a challenge.

#### 4.4.2 REST
- RESTful APIs follow client-server architecture, stateless, use standard HTTP methods (GET, POST, PUT, PATCH, DELETE). Resources identified by URI.
- Advantages: scalable, lightweight, language-agnostic.
- Use in ERDS can bring lightweight access to AS4 nodes or for ERDS evidence repositories.

## 5 Assessment of emerging technologies and models for ERDS/REM provision
- **Commonalities among monitored models** (clause 4.3):
  - Focus on European and international interoperability.
  - When specific electronic delivery technology is recommended/mandated, choice is AS4 (especially DEP eDelivery).
- **Notable elements** (eIDAS 2.0 and NIS2 are covered in part 1 [i.13]):
  - Interoperability addressed by Baseline concept (REM and HTTP baseline) sharing common model.
  - Standardized support for eIDAS 2.0 trust services (especially qualified level) and NIS2 requirements is a serious technical goal not fully achieved by DEP eDelivery alone.
  - Modular component-based approach is crucial to reduce cost of introducing new technologies; impact can be confined.
- **End-user viewpoint**: Base DEP eDelivery widely deployed; full compliance with ETSI standards requires investments in end-user applications. An option is a **"detached" mode** for ERDS evidence management: complexity on provider side, end users retrieve evidence when needed. It is envisaged to standardize an **Evidence component** that can evolve generic electronic delivery or DEP eDelivery to an ERDS/QERDS.
- **NIS2 implementation**: Article 24(1) requires Member States to encourage use of qualified trust services (including QERDS). A robust path from base delivery to (Q)ERDS is important for compliance.
- **Checklists**: Defining checklists as part of standardization supports the component-based approach, aids audit of each component, and helps QERDS providers assess compliance gaps.
