# RFC 3979: Intellectual Property Rights in IETF Technology
**Source**: IETF | **Version**: BCP 79 (March 2005) | **Date**: March 2005 | **Type**: Best Current Practice (Normative)
**Original**: https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc3979

## Scope (Summary)
This document defines IETF policies regarding Intellectual Property Rights (IPR), primarily patent rights, covering technology developed in the IETF. It aims to maximize disclosure of IPR constraints to working groups and participants, benefit the Internet community, and respect IPR holders' legitimate rights. It updates RFC 2026 and, with RFC 3978, replaces Section 10 of RFC 2026. It also updates paragraph 4 of Section 3.2 of RFC 2028. This is not legal advice.

## Definitions (Section 1)
- **IETF**: All individuals participating in activities organized or initiated by ISOC, IESG, or IAB under the IETF designation, solely to the extent of such participation.
- **IETF Contribution**: Any submission intended for publication as all or part of an Internet-Draft or RFC (except RFC Editor Contributions) and any statement made within an IETF activity (oral, written, electronic) addressed to IETF plenary, working group, IESG, IAB, mailing lists, RFC Editor, or Internet-Drafts function. Statements clearly not intended as input to IETF activity are not Contributions.
- **Internet-Draft**: Temporary documents used in IETF and RFC Editor processes, posted by IETF Secretariat, with nominal max lifetime of 6 months.
- **RFC**: Basic publication series for the IETF, published by RFC Editor and never modified.
- **RFC Editor Contribution**: Internet-Draft intended for Informational or Experimental RFC but not part of IETF Standards Process.
- **IETF Internet-Drafts**: Internet-Drafts other than RFC Editor Contributions; rights granted are perpetual and irrevocable.
- **IETF Documents**: RFCs and Internet-Drafts except RFC Editor Contributions and resulting RFCs.
- **RFC Editor Documents**: RFC Editor Contributions and resulting RFCs.
- **Contribution**: IETF Contributions or RFC Editor Contributions.
- **Contributor**: Individual submitting a Contribution.
- **Reasonably and personally known**: Something an individual knows personally or would reasonably be expected to know due to job; does not require a patent search.
- **Implementing Technology**: Technology that implements an IETF specification or standard.
- **Covers / Covered**: A valid claim of a patent or patent application (or other IPR) that would necessarily be infringed by exercise of a right (making, using, selling, etc.) with respect to an Implementing Technology. "Valid claim" means not withdrawn, cancelled, disclaimed, or held invalid in an unappealed decision.
- **IPR / Intellectual Property Rights**: Patent, copyright, utility model, invention registration, database and data rights that may Cover an Implementing Technology.

## Introduction (Section 2 — Condensed Informative)
The IETF follows three basic principles from RFC 2026, Section 10: (a) no determination of IPR validity; (b) the IETF may decide to use technology with IPR if warranted; (c) all Contributors and participants must disclose any IPR they reasonably and personally know that Cover or may Cover technology under discussion. This document clarifies these principles and procedural gaps. A companion document (RFC 3978) covers copyright and trademark rights in Contributions.

## 3. Contributions to the IETF
### 3.1 General Policy
In all matters of IPR, the intent is to benefit the Internet community and public at large while respecting legitimate rights of others.

### 3.2 Rights and Permissions
#### 3.2.1 All Contributions
By submission, each Contributor (including named co-Contributors) is deemed to agree:
- **A**: Contributor represents that he/she has made or will promptly make all disclosures required by Section 6.1.1.
- **B**: Contributor represents no limits to ability to make grants, acknowledgments, and agreements reasonably and personally known.
- **C**: If the Contribution is an Internet-Draft, this agreement must be acknowledged by including appropriate notices per Section 5 of [RFC3978] in the "Status of this Memo" section.

## 4. Actions for Documents for which IPR Disclosure(s) Have Been Received
- **(A)**: When any IPR is disclosed before RFC publication, the RFC Editor **shall** ensure the RFC includes a note indicating the existence of such claimed IPR (see Section 5).
- **(B)**: The IESG disclaims any responsibility for identifying existence or evaluating applicability of any IPR; will take no position on validity or scope.
- **(C)**: Where IPR disclosures have been made for IETF Documents, the IETF Executive Director **shall** request from the discloser a written assurance that all persons can obtain rights to implement, use, distribute, etc. under one of the licensing options in Section 6.5. The results **shall not** block publication or advancement; working group may consider results; IESG may defer approval; results recorded and made available online.

### 4.1 No Determination of Reasonable and Non-discriminatory Terms
The IESG will not explicitly determine that RAND terms have been fulfilled. It will presume terms reasonable if two unrelated implementations exist (Proposed → Draft) or significant implementation/operational experience (Draft → Standard). This presumption may be challenged at any time, including during Last-Call.

## 5. Notice to be Included in RFCs
The RFC Editor **will** ensure the following notice is present in all RFCs for which an IPR disclosure was received before publication:
> "The IETF takes no position regarding the validity or scope of any Intellectual Property Rights or other rights that might be claimed to pertain to the implementation or use of the technology described in this document or the extent to which any license under such rights might or might not be available; nor does it represent that it has made any independent effort to identify any such rights. Information on the procedures with respect to rights in RFC documents can be found in BCP 78 and BCP 79.
>
> Copies of IPR disclosures made to the IETF Secretariat and any assurances of licenses to be made available, or the result of an attempt made to obtain a general license or permission for the use of such proprietary rights by implementers or users of this specification can be obtained from the IETF on-line IPR repository at http://www.ietf.org/ipr.
>
> The IETF invites any interested party to bring to its attention any copyrights, patents or patent applications, or other proprietary rights that may cover technology that may be required to implement this standard. Please address the information to the IETF at ietf-ipr@ietf.org."

## 6. IPR Disclosures
This section details obligations. A participant's obligation is satisfied if the IPR owner or employer/sponsor makes an appropriate disclosure.

### 6.1 Who Must Make an IPR Disclosure?
#### 6.1.1 A Contributor's IPR in his/her Contribution
Any Contributor who reasonably and personally knows of IPR meeting Section 6.6 conditions that *the Contributor believes Covers or may ultimately Cover his/her Contribution* or that the employer/sponsor may assert against Implementing Technologies, **must** disclose. This includes electronic or spoken comments; if rejected from consideration, disclosure may be deferred. Contributors **must** disclose regardless of exceptions.

#### 6.1.2 An IETF Participant's IPR in Contributions by Others
Any participant who reasonably and personally knows of IPR meeting Section 6.6 conditions that *the individual believes Covers or may ultimately Cover a Contribution by another* or that employer/sponsor may assert, **must** disclose.

#### 6.1.3 IPR of Others
If a person has information about IPR not meeting Section 6.6 criteria (e.g., owned by another company), they are **encouraged** to notify the IETF at ietf-ipr@ietf.org as soon as reasonably possible.

### 6.2 Timing of Disclosure
#### 6.2.1 Timing under Section 6.1.1
Disclosure **must** be made as soon as reasonably possible after the Contribution is published in an Internet-Draft, unless already on file. If a Contributor later learns of new IPR, disclosure **must** be made as soon as reasonably possible after becoming known. Participants are **strongly encouraged** to make preliminary disclosure as soon as they realize a Contribution will be incorporated.

#### 6.2.2 Timing under Section 6.1.2
Disclosure **must** be made as soon as reasonably possible after the Contribution is published in an Internet-Draft or RFC, unless already on file. Participants are **strongly encouraged** to make preliminary disclosure as soon as they realize IPR will be incorporated.

### 6.3 How Must a Disclosure be Made?
By following instructions at http://www.ietf.org/ipr-instructions.

### 6.4 What Must be in a Disclosure?
#### 6.4.1
**Must** list numbers of issued patents or published patent applications (or indicate unpublished). **Must** list specific IETF or RFC Editor Document(s) or activity, including specific version number for Internet-Drafts. Helpful to identify sections alleged to be covered.

#### 6.4.2
If disclosure based on patent application, the IESG or working group chair may request that the IETF Executive Director request a new disclosure after publication, abandonment, or issuance. If patent issued, new disclosure **must** include patent number (differences helpful). If abandoned, new disclosure **must** explicitly withdraw earlier disclosures.

#### 6.4.3
Blanket statements of possible IPR on every Contribution do **not** satisfy the requirement. A blanket statement of willingness to license all potential IPR on a royalty-free basis (with terms disclosed) does satisfy, if the IPR meets Sections 6.6 and either 6.1.1 or 6.1.2.

### 6.5 What Licensing Information to Detail in a Disclosure
It is **helpful** to indicate whether all persons can obtain rights to implement, use, distribute, etc. under: (a) royalty-free and otherwise reasonable and non-discriminatory license; (b) RAND license with reasonable royalty; or (c) without a license. Inclusion is not mandatory but encouraged.

### 6.6 When is a Disclosure Required?
Required with respect to IPR owned directly/indirectly by the individual, employer/sponsor, or that such persons have the right to license or assert.

## 7. Failure to Disclose
A person who is not permitted to disclose IPR (e.g., by employer) **must not** contribute to or participate in IETF activities with respect to technologies they reasonably and personally know to be covered by IPR they will not disclose. Doing so is a violation of IETF process.

## 8. Evaluating Alternative Technologies in IETF Working Groups
IETF working groups generally prefer technologies with no known IPR claims or royalty-free licensing. They have discretion to adopt technology with RAND terms or no licensing commitment if superior. For mandatory-to-implement security technologies, consensus requires no known IPR claims or royalty-free license unless very good reason. Absence of disclosures does not guarantee future freedom; validity/enforceability may be challenged.

## 9. Change Control for Technologies
The IETF must have change control over technology in standards track IETF Documents. There is no inherent prohibition against normative references to proprietary technology (e.g., cryptographic transforms).

## 10. Licensing Requirements to Advance Standards Track IETF Documents
Section 4.1.3 of RFC 2026 states: "If patented or otherwise controlled technology is required for implementation, the separate implementations must also have resulted from separate exercise of the licensing process." This applies only if licenses are actually required. If multiple implementers have not felt the need to license and no plausible enforcement indication, the requirement is satisfied.

## 11. No IPR Disclosures in IETF Documents
IETF and RFC Editor Documents **must not** contain any mention of specific IPR. All specific disclosures must be submitted as per Section 6. Readers should refer to the online IPR repository at http://www.ietf.org/ipr/.

## 12. Security Considerations
No known security issues with IPR procedures; working groups should consider security when evaluating technology.
