Today's verification processes simply weren't built for AI generated documents that can be produced quickly, at scale, and tested across multiple organisations until one passes review.

AI-generated documents at scale are forcing a shift in how we think about trust infrastructure.

Strengthening issuance and moving toward verifiable credentials is a big step forward.

Where it gets harder is what happens after that.

Even with valid credentials, you can still end up with multiple internally consistent versions of what happened across systems, timelines, or transformed copies.

For example:
A legitimate digital death certificate is issued and verifies correctly.

But downstream:
- a screenshot is submitted through a claims workflow
- a re-encoded version is ingested into another system
- a backend record still shows the individual as active due to lag or fragmentation

Each piece is explainable. None fully reconcile.

At that point the problem shifts from verification to adjudication:
not “is this authentic?”
but “what actually holds up when records conflict?”

Feels like there’s a missing layer between identity infrastructure and real-world dispute resolution — especially in fraud scenarios where conflicting records are the norm, not the exception.

(Not an April Fool's post)

The sooner we get a handle on this problem, the better.

For example, if I haven't looked at my 401(k) for a few months, how can the provider find out someone claiming my death is authentic or not, based on the image of a death certificate they have produced. The entire image-based proof process we have for authenticity verification is at serious risk due to AI.

I think for the respective authorities to be able to issue "Digital death certificates", or for hospitals or doctors to issue "digital incapacitation certificates" will work as a solution, but (AFAIK) we do not have any infrastructure right now in that direction. The potential for fraud using fake certificate images is enormous, especially for infrequent users. We need to get on this quickly.

As
Juliana Cafik
eloquently said "We need to move from pixels to proofs" for verification. Digital certificates are sound cryptographic proofs.

Great work,
Dean H. Saxe
and
Mike Kiser
in highlighting the issues through the DADE group in the
OpenID Foundation
